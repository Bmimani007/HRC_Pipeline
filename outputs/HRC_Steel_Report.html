Lead-lag analysis decomposes each driver's co-movement with HRC across lags from −6 to +6 months, computed on STL residuals — the idiosyncratic 'surprise' component of each series after removing trend and 12-month seasonality. This isolates true short-run transmission from shared trends and seasonal pulses (e.g. spring construction season) that otherwise mask real lead-lag signals. A positive lag means the driver moves first and HRC follows (a leading indicator); a negative lag means HRC moves first and the driver follows (a lagging indicator); lag zero indicates a coincident relationship. Nov 2021 Steel Supercycle Peak (Nov 2021 · HRC move -13.5% over ±6m)

What changed:

  • China HRC FOB rolled over from its cycle high; Chinese rebar/wire-rod futures fell roughly 24-31% from the mid-September peak into Q4.
  • China crude-steel output fell more than 20% year-on-year from September 2021 as Beijing enforced output caps.
  • Iron ore had already crashed from its May 2021 record (~$230/t spot) toward ~$90-120/t by Q4 on collapsing mill demand.
  • Coking coal stayed extremely elevated on tight seaborne supply, squeezing mill margins from both sides.

Why it changed: The 2021 peak was the exhaustion of a post-COVID restocking surge running straight into a deliberate Chinese policy brake. Beijing ordered crude-steel output held at or below 2020 levels (a decarbonisation goal — steel is roughly 15% of China's carbon emissions), which curbed production from July 2021 onward. At the same time China's property sector — about 28% of GDP and the single largest steel end-market — was contracting: new construction starts by floor area had been falling year-on-year since July 2021, the longest run of declines since 2015, and the Evergrande developer-default crisis crystallised through late 2021. Demand expectations rolled over while coking-coal input costs stayed high, so mill margins compressed and the price cycle turned down.

Country breakdown:

  • CHINA — Epicentre. Output caps plus a property-demand contraction hit HRC and iron ore together. Chinese steel equities fell ~27% and construction- steel futures 24-31% from the September peak. China led the downturn.
  • INDIA — More resilient and later. Domestic demand and the festival/construction cycle cushioned the move; INR HRC corrected less and lagged China FOB.
  • US — A different clock. US Midwest HRC stayed historically elevated into 2022 on local supply tightness and import barriers — the US peak lagged China's by months.

Tracing the drivers:

10-Year Treasury Yield (%) — The US 10-year Treasury yield rose materially from late 2021 into 2022 as the Federal Reserve pivoted to fighting inflation.

With US producer prices at multi-year highs, the Fed signalled faster stimulus tapering and then rate hikes through late 2021 and into 2022, pushing the 10-year yield up. This is a pure macro/monetary driver: it reflects US inflation and Fed policy, not steel fundamentals. In the decomposition it often shows a large raw beta because it is a small- magnitude series (a few percent) regressed on a price in the hundreds — the scale-aware |beta x sigma| weighting corrects for this, but the driver's CAUSAL link to HRC is indirect (it works through the dollar and through global demand conditions, not through the steel market).

Where the trigger sat: United States — the trigger is entirely US monetary policy. Treat its decomposition contribution as a macro-conditions proxy, not a steel cause.

Crude Oil — Crude oil rose through most of 2021 (Brent ~$52 to ~$86) but wobbled into year-end as the Omicron variant briefly hit demand expectations.

The 2021 oil rally was a post-COVID demand recovery meeting OPEC+ supply restraint — the cartel deliberately added barrels slowly while US shale held back on investor pressure. The late-November Omicron scare cut prices ~16% in days on fears of renewed travel restrictions, before a partial recovery as the variant proved milder than feared. For HRC, oil matters as an energy-input and freight-cost proxy rather than a direct driver — it moved on its own oil-market supply/demand balance, only loosely coupled to the steel cycle.

Where the trigger sat: Global / OPEC+ — the trigger was the OPEC+ supply policy and the global Omicron demand scare, not anything specific to the steel market.

Iron Ore 62%Fe (China CFR) — Iron ore crashed from a record ~$230/t (May 2021) toward $90-120/t by Q4 2021 — one of its sharpest declines on record.

The fall was almost entirely a China demand shock, not a supply event. Beijing's order to hold 2021 crude-steel output at/below 2020 levels forced mills to cut production from July; blast-furnace utilisation at 247 mills fell well below the prior year. Anti-pollution restrictions in Tangshan — the city that alone accounts for about a quarter of Chinese steel output — repeatedly curbed mill operations. With mills cutting runs, demand for the raw material collapsed even as Australian and Brazilian shipments continued. Chinese regulators (NDRC) also cracked down on iron-ore speculation, adding downward pressure.

Where the trigger sat: China — the trigger was entirely Chinese policy (output caps, Tangshan pollution curbs, NDRC anti-speculation action). Australia and Brazil, the supply side, played no triggering role in the fall.

BDI — The Baltic Dry Index peaked in October 2021 at multi-year highs, then fell sharply into late 2021 and early 2022.

The 2021 BDI spike was a collision of strong dry-bulk commodity demand (iron ore, coal) with pandemic-era port congestion and a tight bulk- carrier fleet — capacity could not be added fast enough. The October peak unwound as Chinese steel-output cuts curbed iron-ore shipping demand and port congestion gradually eased. BDI also tracks bunker-fuel (oil) cost, so it carried some of the energy-price signal too. As a driver of HRC it is a coincident demand/freight gauge, not a lead cause.

Where the trigger sat: China-led on the demand side (iron-ore output cuts reduced shipping demand); global on the congestion side. Not a steel-market trigger in itself.

Iron and Steel Scrap — Ferrous scrap broadly tracked the finished-steel cycle — elevated in 2021, easing through the 2022 correction, with stimulus-driven wobbles in 2024.

Scrap is an alternative metallic input to iron ore (used heavily in electric-arc-furnace steelmaking). Its price tends to move WITH the steel cycle rather than lead it: when finished-steel demand and prices are strong, mills bid up scrap; when the cycle turns, scrap follows down. Around all three events scrap behaved as a coincident input-cost indicator rather than an independent trigger.

Where the trigger sat: Demand-following — no single-country trigger; scrap reflects the same steel-cycle forces as HRC itself.

DXY — The US dollar index strengthened from late 2021 into 2022 alongside the Fed's hawkish turn.

The dollar rose as the Fed moved ahead of other major central banks on tightening, and again as the Ukraine war drove safe-haven demand. A stronger dollar mechanically pressures dollar-priced commodities, including iron ore, since it raises the local-currency cost for buyers outside the US. For HRC, DXY is a macro/financial transmission channel (USD to commodity prices), not a steel-demand driver.

Where the trigger sat: United States — driven by Fed policy and global safe-haven flows.

  • Thermal and metallurgical coal hit record highs; freight and energy costs jumped on the war premium.
  • HRC saw a brief sympathy spike, then resumed a broad multi-quarter decline as demand worry took over.

Why it changed: The invasion was a supply shock layered on an already-cooling demand backdrop. The war removed supply and spiked input costs, briefly lifting steel prices. But the dominant medium-term force was demand: surging energy costs fed global inflation, central banks began aggressive rate hikes, and the Chinese property slowdown continued. After the initial shock spike, HRC followed input costs and demand expectations back down, extending the correction that had begun after the Nov 2021 peak.

Nov 2021 Steel Supercycle Peak (Nov 2021 · HRC move -26.8% over ±6m)

What changed:

  • China HRC FOB rolled over from its cycle high; Chinese rebar/wire-rod futures fell roughly 24-31% from the mid-September peak into Q4.
  • China crude-steel output fell more than 20% year-on-year from September 2021 as Beijing enforced output caps.
  • Iron ore had already crashed from its May 2021 record (~$230/t spot) toward ~$90-120/t by Q4 on collapsing mill demand.
  • Coking coal stayed extremely elevated on tight seaborne supply, squeezing mill margins from both sides.

Why it changed: The 2021 peak was the exhaustion of a post-COVID restocking surge running straight into a deliberate Chinese policy brake. Beijing ordered crude-steel output held at or below 2020 levels (a decarbonisation goal — steel is roughly 15% of China's carbon emissions), which curbed production from July 2021 onward. At the same time China's property sector — about 28% of GDP and the single largest steel end-market — was contracting: new construction starts by floor area had been falling year-on-year since July 2021, the longest run of declines since 2015, and the Evergrande developer-default crisis crystallised through late 2021. Demand expectations rolled over while coking-coal input costs stayed high, so mill margins compressed and the price cycle turned down.

Country breakdown:

  • CHINA — Epicentre. Output caps plus a property-demand contraction hit HRC and iron ore together. Chinese steel equities fell ~27% and construction- steel futures 24-31% from the September peak. China led the downturn.
  • INDIA — More resilient and later. Domestic demand and the festival/construction cycle cushioned the move; INR HRC corrected less and lagged China FOB.
  • US — A different clock. US Midwest HRC stayed historically elevated into 2022 on local supply tightness and import barriers — the US peak lagged China's by months.

Ukraine War & Multi-Year Correction (Feb 2022 · HRC move -32.6% over ±6m)

What changed:

  • Russia invaded Ukraine on 24 February 2022, triggering an immediate spike in energy and bulk-commodity prices.
  • Australian premium hard coking coal surged toward and past US$500/t — up roughly 140% year-on-year by end-March 2022.
  • Thermal and metallurgical coal hit record highs; freight and energy costs jumped on the war premium.
  • HRC saw a brief sympathy spike, then resumed a broad multi-quarter decline as demand worry took over.

Why it changed: The invasion was a supply shock layered on an already-cooling demand backdrop. Russia is a major energy, coal and pig-iron supplier, and Ukraine itself was a meaningful iron-ore and steel exporter (its crude- steel output fell roughly 68% in 2022 as plants such as Azovstal in Mariupol were destroyed). The war removed supply and spiked input costs, briefly lifting steel prices. But the dominant medium-term force was demand: surging energy costs fed global inflation, central banks began aggressive rate hikes, and the Chinese property slowdown continued. After the initial shock spike, HRC followed input costs and demand expectations back down, extending the correction that had begun after the Nov 2021 peak.

Country breakdown:

  • CHINA — Demand-led weakness continued; the war's input-cost spike was a short- lived overlay on an already-downward trajectory.
  • INDIA — Hit from both sides. India imports ~85% of its coking coal, so the coal spike fed directly into mill costs — domestic producers raised HRC and TMT prices by up to Rs 5,000/t in early March 2022. Then India's May 2022 steel export duty redirected supply to the domestic market and weighed on Indian HRC into mid-2022.
  • US — Energy-cost and inflation channel dominant. US HRC rolled over through 2022 as Fed tightening hit construction and manufacturing demand.
